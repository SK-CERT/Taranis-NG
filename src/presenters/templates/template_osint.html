Terminology

Threat level is calculated based on the average of the trends observed. ENISA adopts a 5-level method for describing Threat Levels, which is commonly used by ISACs and working groups. Threat level is on a rolling-month window interval.

SEVERE It is almost certain that organisations are being targeted by threat actors. High severity vulnerabilities with no known remediation are being exploited and significant damage and outages are being observed across sectors.
HIGH It is highly likely that entities will be directly targeted by threat actors. Multiple entities will be, or are being, affected. Sector disruption is expected to be widespread, across multiple organisations.
ELEVATED It is likely that entities are being directly targeted by threat actors or could be exposed to breaches using recent vulnerabilities. Sector disruption is considered a realistic possibility.
GUARDED There is potential for some direct targeted threat actor activity but it is generally considered Unlikely. This activity could lead to some minor disruption.
LOW A low likelihood of threat actor targeting activity that could affect organisations/entities. Disruption is considered highly unlikely.

Statistics and dashboards

The displayed data is based on a one-month rolling window. The clustered bar charts on incidents per sector and threat type display the average number of incidents compared to the current week's one.

Trends Legend

Trends Description

High Increase 		The number of incidents related to this sector/threat, shows a sustained and significant upward movement over time.

Increase 		The number of incidents related to this sector/threat, shows a sustained upward movement over time, but at a slower pace than a high increase.

Stable 		The number of incidents related to this sector/threat shows little to no change over time.

Low Decrease 		The number of incidents related to this sector/threat, shows a sustained downward movement over time, but at a slower pace than a decrease.

Decrease 		The number of incidents related to this sector/threat, shows a sustained and significant downward movement over time.

Event Severity

The potential impact that the event has caused/could cause to the organisation, such as physical damage, economic damage, reputational damage, psychological, social/societal. According to the ENISA event taxonomy, the potential impact can be characterised with the following values: Low, Medium, High or Unknown.

HIGH An incident that has the potential to cause significant harm to the system or organization, with high impact on data or services.
MEDIUM An incident that has the potential to cause moderate harm to the system or organization, with moderate impact on data or services.
LOW An incident that has the potential to cause minimal harm to the system or organization, with low impact on data or services.
UNKNOWN An incident where there is not enough information to determine the severity level, or where the incident is still being investigated.

Source confidence

It defines the level of certainty that the event is genuine and not a false positive. This is based on a two-character notation of the Admiralty system, characterising the reliability of the source and the credibility of the information.

Source Reliability Information Credibility
A - Completely reliable 1 - Confirmed by other sources
B - Usually reliable 2 - Probably True
C - Fairly reliable 3 - Possibly True
D - Not usually reliable 4 - Doubtful
E - Unreliable 5 - Improbable
F - Reliability cannot be judged 6 - Truth cannot be judged
